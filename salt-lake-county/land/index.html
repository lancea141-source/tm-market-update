What the data shows

Size elasticity of 0.638. Doubling lot size raises price about 56%, so price per acre falls roughly 22% with each doubling. Buyers pay for the right to build, not for raw ground.
345 of 471 sales closed below their original asking price, at a median total discount of 9.4%. Sale-to-last-list ratios near 100% are a reporting artifact of Utah non-disclosure — they measure the price after reductions, not the price the seller started at.
Current inventory is priced +10.9% against the model. Of 144 active listings in scope, 68 are more than 15% above modeled value. That overhang is the pipeline for the next round of expired listings.

Why large parcels are excluded

This market contains two kinds of land that do not price the same way, and only one of them can be modeled reliably from public sales data.

Under 2 acres, price follows size, location, services and lot features in a predictable curve. Above it the relationship breaks down — these are mostly unserviced parcels on the fringe, priced on entitlement risk and utility distance rather than on acreage. A parcel in that range needs individual analysis, not a model. If you own acreage that has not sold, that is the reason, and it is fixable with the right approach.

Method

Log-linear regression on 378 sales, Jul 2023 to Jul 2026. Predictors: lot size (log), city, utility availability, months elapsed, and 9 lot features (View: Mountain, View: Valley, Cul-de-Sac, Curb & Gutter, Sidewalks, Sloped Terrain, Flat Terrain, Fenced, Horse Property). R² = 0.427. Median absolute error 22.4%. 15 outliers removed at 2.5× IQR on price per acre. Cities with fewer than 8 sales are pooled. Source: WFRMLS/URE closed sales.
